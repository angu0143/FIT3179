Youth Crime in Australia

Introduction

This dashboard visualises youth crime in Australia, focusing on how crime rates vary across different states and offence categories. Understanding patterns of youth offending is critical for policymakers, researchers, and communities who aim to address the underlying social, economic, and cultural factors contributing to youth involvement in crime. This visualisation provides a clear and interactive way to examine these trends by transforming complex crime data into an accessible, visual format. It allows users to compare offence rates between states, identify which types of offences are most prevalent among young people, and observe any regional disparities.

Youth Crimes Detected Across Australia

This map visualises the distribution of youth offences detected across Australian states and territories, showing the number of youth offenders by offence type and location. Each state is represented according to the level and type of youth crime recorded, allowing for easy comparison across regions.

The data shows that offences such as Acts Intended to Cause Injury, Theft, and Public Order Offences are the most frequently detected among young people, particularly in New South Wales, Queensland, and Victoria. In contrast, serious offences like Homicide and Abduction/Harassment occur far less often and are scattered across fewer states.

The overall map highlights that youth crime is most concentrated in the eastern states, with smaller totals in the Northern Territory, South Australia, and the Australian Capital Territory, providing a clear visual summary of where and which types of youth offences are most prominent across Australia.

Youth Offenders by Age & Sex

The line chart reveals a clear and consistent pattern in youth offender rates by age and sex between 2022 and 2023. Offending behaviour increases sharply with age for both males and females, peaking around mid- to late-adolescence. For males, the offender rate climbs rapidly from 197 per 100,000 at age 10 to a peak of 4,354.9 per 100,000 at age 16, before slightly declining at age 17. Females show a similar upward trend, though at much lower levels, rising from 66.3 per 100,000 at age 10 to 1,816.9 per 100,000 at age 14, followed by moderate fluctuations through the later teenage years. Across all ages, male youth offenders significantly outnumber females, indicating a persistent gender gap in offending patterns.

These results suggest that youth crime involvement intensifies through early adolescence; potentially linked to social, socioeconomic, and peer influences, before stabilising as individuals approach adulthood. The sharper escalation among males may reflect differences in behavioural risk factors, socialisation, and exposure to criminogenic environments.

Youth Offenders Detention Rate

Notably, there is a stark disparity in youth detention rates between Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander and Non-Indigenous populations across Australian states and territories. Overall, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander youths are detained at significantly higher rates in every jurisdiction. The highest detention rates are observed in Queensland (46.0 per 10,000) and the Northern Territory (42.9 per 10,000), followed closely by Western Australia and the Australian Capital Territory (both at 34.6 per 10,000).

In contrast, Victoria and Tasmania record comparatively lower rates, though these figures remain many times higher than those of Non-Indigenous youths. Across all states, Non-Indigenous youth detention rates remain consistently low, below 2.5 per 10,000, illustrating a persistent and systemic over-representation of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander youths in detention. This pattern underscores enduring inequalities in the justice system and reflects broader social, economic, and cultural disadvantages experienced by Indigenous communities nationwide.

Socioeconomic Advantage vs Youth Offence Rate

The scatter plot visualisation highlights a negative correlation between socioeconomic advantage (measured by SEIFA) and youth offence rates across Australian states and territories. States with lower average SEIFA scores, such as the Northern Territory (875) and Queensland (963), record the highest youth offence rates (2855 and 1863 per 100,000 youths respectively). In contrast, states with higher SEIFA scores, notably the Australian Capital Territory (1,089) and Victoria (997), show substantially lower offence rates (528.9 and 1,126.6 per 100,000 youths).

This pattern suggests that regions with greater socioeconomic disadvantage tend to experience higher levels of youth offending, reflecting the influence of structural inequality, limited educational and employment opportunities, and social marginalisation on crime involvement. The trend reinforces criminological theories linking socioeconomic stressors to delinquency and demonstrates how youth justice outcomes are strongly shaped by broader social and economic conditions across Australia.
